84  <glossentry id="glossary-aio">
85   <glossterm>Asynchronous <acronym>I/O</acronym></glossterm>
86   <acronym>AIO</acronym>
87   <indexterm>
88    <primary>Asynchronous <acronym>I/O</acronym></primary>
89   </indexterm>
90   <glossdef>
91    <para>
92     Asynchronous <acronym>I/O</acronym> (<acronym>AIO</acronym>) describes
93     performing <acronym>I/O</acronym> in a non-blocking way (asynchronously),
94     in contrast to synchronous <acronym>I/O</acronym>, which blocks for the
95     entire duration of the <acronym>I/O</acronym>.
96    </para>
97    <para>
98     With <acronym>AIO</acronym>, starting an <acronym>I/O</acronym> operation
99     is separated from waiting for the result of the operation, allowing
100     multiple <acronym>I/O</acronym> operations to be initiated concurrently,
101     as well as performing <acronym>CPU</acronym> heavy operations
102     concurrently with <acronym>I/O</acronym>. The price for that increased
103     concurrency is increased complexity.
104    </para>
105    <glossseealso otherterm="glossary-io" />
106   </glossdef>

297  <glossentry id="glossary-buffer-access-strategy">
298   <glossterm>Buffer Access Strategy</glossterm>
299   <glossdef>
300    <para>
301     Some operations will access a large number of
302     <glossterm linkend="glossary-data-page">pages</glossterm>.  A
303     <firstterm>Buffer Access Strategy</firstterm> helps to prevent these
304     operations from evicting too many pages from
305     <glossterm linkend="glossary-shared-memory">shared buffers</glossterm>.
306    </para>
307    <para>
308     A Buffer Access Strategy sets up references to a limited number of
309     <glossterm linkend="glossary-shared-memory">shared buffers</glossterm> and
310     reuses them circularly.  When the operation requires a new page, a victim
311     buffer is chosen from the buffers in the strategy ring, which may require
312     flushing the page's dirty data and possibly also unflushed
313     <glossterm linkend="glossary-wal">WAL</glossterm> to permanent storage.
314    </para>
315    <para>
316     Buffer Access Strategies are used for various operations such as
317     sequential scans of large tables, <command>VACUUM</command>,
318     <command>COPY</command>, <command>CREATE TABLE AS SELECT</command>,
319     <command>ALTER TABLE</command>, <command>CREATE DATABASE</command>,
320     <command>CREATE INDEX</command>, and <command>CLUSTER</command>.
321    </para>
322   </glossdef>

378  <glossentry id="glossary-checkpoint">
379   <glossterm>Checkpoint</glossterm>
380   <glossdef>
381    <para>
382     A point in the <glossterm linkend="glossary-wal">WAL</glossterm> sequence
383     at which it is guaranteed that the heap and index data files have been
384     updated with all information from
385     <glossterm linkend="glossary-shared-memory">shared memory</glossterm>

391     A checkpoint is also the act of carrying out all the actions that
392     are necessary to reach a checkpoint as defined above.
393     This process is initiated when predefined conditions are met,
394     such as a specified amount of time has passed, or a certain volume
395     of records has been written; or it can be invoked by the user
396     with the command <command>CHECKPOINT</command>.
397    </para>
