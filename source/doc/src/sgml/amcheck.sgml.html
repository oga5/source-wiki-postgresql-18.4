3<sect1 id="amcheck" xreflabel="amcheck">
4 <title>amcheck &mdash; tools to verify table and index consistency</title>
5
6 <indexterm zone="amcheck">
7  <primary>amcheck</primary>
8 </indexterm>
9
10 <para>
11  The <filename>amcheck</filename> module provides functions that allow you to
12  verify the logical consistency of the structure of relations.
13 </para>
14
15 <para>
16  The B-Tree checking functions verify various <emphasis>invariants</emphasis> in the
17  structure of the representation of particular relations.  The
18  correctness of the access method functions behind index scans and
19  other important operations relies on these invariants always
20  holding.  For example, certain functions verify, among other things,
21  that all B-Tree pages have items in <quote>logical</quote> order (e.g.,
22  for B-Tree indexes on <type>text</type>, index tuples should be in
23  collated lexical order).  If that particular invariant somehow fails
24  to hold, we can expect binary searches on the affected page to
25  incorrectly guide index scans, resulting in wrong answers to SQL
26  queries.  If the structure appears to be valid, no error is raised.
27  While these checking functions are run, the <xref
28  linkend="guc-search-path"/> is temporarily changed to <literal>pg_catalog,
29  pg_temp</literal>.
30 </para>
31 <para>
32  Verification is performed using the same procedures as those used by
33  index scans themselves, which may be user-defined operator class
34  code.  For example, B-Tree index verification relies on comparisons
35  made with one or more B-Tree support function 1 routines.  See <xref
36  linkend="xindex-support"/> for details of operator class support
37  functions.
38 </para>
39 <para>
40  Unlike the B-Tree checking functions which report corruption by raising
41  errors, the heap checking function <function>verify_heapam</function> checks
42  a table and attempts to return a set of rows, one row per corruption
43  detected.  Despite this, if facilities that
44  <function>verify_heapam</function> relies upon are themselves corrupted, the
45  function may be unable to continue and may instead raise an error.
46 </para>
47 <para>
48  Permission to execute <filename>amcheck</filename> functions may be granted
49  to non-superusers, but before granting such permissions careful consideration
50  should be given to data security and privacy concerns.  Although the
51  corruption reports generated by these functions do not focus on the contents
52  of the corrupted data so much as on the structure of that data and the nature
53  of the corruptions found, an attacker who gains permission to execute these
54  functions, particularly if the attacker can also induce corruption, might be
55  able to infer something of the data itself from such messages.
56 </para>

61  <variablelist>

63    <term>
64     <function>bt_index_check(index regclass, heapallindexed boolean, checkunique boolean) returns void</function>
65     <indexterm>
66      <primary>bt_index_check</primary>
67     </indexterm>
68    </term>
69
70    <listitem>
71     <para>
72      <function>bt_index_check</function> tests that its target, a
73      B-Tree index, respects a variety of invariants.  Example usage:
74<screen>
75test=# SELECT bt_index_check(index =&gt; c.oid, heapallindexed =&gt; i.indisunique),
76               c.relname,
77               c.relpages
78FROM pg_index i
79JOIN pg_opclass op ON i.indclass[0] = op.oid
80JOIN pg_am am ON op.opcmethod = am.oid
81JOIN pg_class c ON i.indexrelid = c.oid
82JOIN pg_namespace n ON c.relnamespace = n.oid
83WHERE am.amname = 'btree' AND n.nspname = 'pg_catalog'
84-- Don't check temp tables, which may be from another session:
85AND c.relpersistence != 't'
86-- Function may throw an error when this is omitted:
87AND c.relkind = 'i' AND i.indisready AND i.indisvalid
88ORDER BY c.relpages DESC LIMIT 10;
89 bt_index_check |             relname             | relpages
90----------------+---------------------------------+----------
91                | pg_depend_reference_index       |       43
92                | pg_depend_depender_index        |       40
93                | pg_proc_proname_args_nsp_index  |       31
94                | pg_description_o_c_o_index      |       21
95                | pg_attribute_relid_attnam_index |       14
96                | pg_proc_oid_index               |       10
97                | pg_attribute_relid_attnum_index |        9
98                | pg_amproc_fam_proc_index        |        5
99                | pg_amop_opr_fam_index           |        5
100                | pg_amop_fam_strat_index         |        5
101(10 rows)
102</screen>
103      This example shows a session that performs verification of the
104      10 largest catalog indexes in the database <quote>test</quote>.
105      Verification of the presence of heap tuples as index tuples is
106      requested for the subset that are unique indexes.  Since no
107      error is raised, all indexes tested appear to be logically
108      consistent.  Naturally, this query could easily be changed to
109      call <function>bt_index_check</function> for every index in the
110      database where verification is supported.
111     </para>
112     <para>
113      <function>bt_index_check</function> acquires an <literal>AccessShareLock</literal>
114      on the target index and the heap relation it belongs to. This lock mode
115      is the same lock mode acquired on relations by simple
116      <literal>SELECT</literal> statements.
117      <function>bt_index_check</function> does not verify invariants
118      that span child/parent relationships, but will verify the
119      presence of all heap tuples as index tuples within the index
120      when <parameter>heapallindexed</parameter> is
121      <literal>true</literal>.  When <parameter>checkunique</parameter>
122      is <literal>true</literal> <function>bt_index_check</function> will
123      check that no more than one among duplicate entries in unique
124      index is visible.  When a routine, lightweight test for
125      corruption is required in a live production environment, using
126      <function>bt_index_check</function> often provides the best
127      trade-off between thoroughness of verification and limiting the
128      impact on application performance and availability.
129     </para>
130    </listitem>
131   </varlistentry>
132
133   <varlistentry>
134    <term>
135     <function>bt_index_parent_check(index regclass, heapallindexed boolean, rootdescend boolean, checkunique boolean) returns void</function>
136     <indexterm>
137      <primary>bt_index_parent_check</primary>
138     </indexterm>
139    </term>
140
141    <listitem>
142     <para>
143      <function>bt_index_parent_check</function> tests that its
144      target, a B-Tree index, respects a variety of invariants.
145      Optionally, when the <parameter>heapallindexed</parameter>
146      argument is <literal>true</literal>, the function verifies the
147      presence of all heap tuples that should be found within the
148      index.  When <parameter>checkunique</parameter>
149      is <literal>true</literal> <function>bt_index_parent_check</function> will
150      check that no more than one among duplicate entries in unique
151      index is visible.  When the optional <parameter>rootdescend</parameter>
152      argument is <literal>true</literal>, verification re-finds
153      tuples on the leaf level by performing a new search from the
154      root page for each tuple.  The checks that can be performed by
155      <function>bt_index_parent_check</function> are a superset of the
156      checks that can be performed by <function>bt_index_check</function>.
157      <function>bt_index_parent_check</function> can be thought of as
158      a more thorough variant of <function>bt_index_check</function>:
159      unlike <function>bt_index_check</function>,
160      <function>bt_index_parent_check</function> also checks
161      invariants that span parent/child relationships, including checking
162      that there are no missing downlinks in the index structure.
163      <function>bt_index_parent_check</function> follows the general
164      convention of raising an error if it finds a logical
165      inconsistency or other problem.
166     </para>
167     <para>
168      A <literal>ShareLock</literal> is required on the target index by
169      <function>bt_index_parent_check</function> (a
170      <literal>ShareLock</literal> is also acquired on the heap relation).
171      These locks prevent concurrent data modification from
172      <command>INSERT</command>, <command>UPDATE</command>, and <command>DELETE</command>
173      commands.  The locks also prevent the underlying relation from
174      being concurrently processed by <command>VACUUM</command>, as well as
175      all other utility commands.  Note that the function holds locks
176      only while running, not for the entire transaction.
177     </para>
178     <para>
179      <function>bt_index_parent_check</function>'s additional
180      verification is more likely to detect various pathological
181      cases.  These cases may involve an incorrectly implemented
182      B-Tree operator class used by the index that is checked, or,
183      hypothetically, undiscovered bugs in the underlying B-Tree index
184      access method code.  Note that
185      <function>bt_index_parent_check</function> cannot be used when
186      hot standby mode is enabled (i.e., on read-only physical
187      replicas), unlike <function>bt_index_check</function>.
188     </para>
189    </listitem>
190   </varlistentry>

202      <function>gin_index_check</function> tests that its target GIN index
203      has consistent parent-child tuples relations (no parent tuples

213   <para>
214    <function>bt_index_check</function> and
215    <function>bt_index_parent_check</function> both output log
216    messages about the verification process at
217    <literal>DEBUG1</literal> and <literal>DEBUG2</literal> severity

219    verification process that may be of interest to
220    <productname>PostgreSQL</productname> developers.  Advanced users

224<programlisting>

229    progress of verification with a manageable level of detail.

236     <function>
237      verify_heapam(relation regclass,
238                    on_error_stop boolean,
239                    check_toast boolean,
240                    skip text,
241                    startblock bigint,
242                    endblock bigint,
243                    blkno OUT bigint,
244                    offnum OUT integer,
245                    attnum OUT integer,
246                    msg OUT text)
247      returns setof record
248     </function>
249    </term>
250    <listitem>
251     <para>
252      Checks a table, sequence, or materialized view for structural corruption,
253      where pages in the relation contain data that is invalidly formatted, and
254      for logical corruption, where pages are structurally valid but
255      inconsistent with the rest of the database cluster.
256     </para>
257     <para>
258      The following optional arguments are recognized:
259     </para>
260     <variablelist>
261      <varlistentry>
262       <term><literal>on_error_stop</literal></term>
263       <listitem>
264        <para>
265         If true, corruption checking stops at the end of the first block in
266         which any corruptions are found.
267        </para>
268        <para>
269         Defaults to false.
270        </para>
271       </listitem>
272      </varlistentry>
273      <varlistentry>
274       <term><literal>check_toast</literal></term>
275       <listitem>
276        <para>
277         If true, toasted values are checked against the target relation's
278         TOAST table.
279        </para>
280        <para>
281         This option is known to be slow.  Also, if the toast table or its
282         index is corrupt, checking it against toast values could conceivably
283         crash the server, although in many cases this would just produce an
284         error.
285        </para>
286        <para>
287         Defaults to false.
288        </para>
289       </listitem>
290      </varlistentry>
291      <varlistentry>
292       <term><literal>skip</literal></term>
293       <listitem>
294        <para>
295         If not <literal>none</literal>, corruption checking skips blocks that
296         are marked as all-visible or all-frozen, as specified.
297         Valid options are <literal>all-visible</literal>,
298         <literal>all-frozen</literal> and <literal>none</literal>.
299        </para>
300        <para>
301         Defaults to <literal>none</literal>.
302        </para>
303       </listitem>
304      </varlistentry>
305      <varlistentry>
306       <term><literal>startblock</literal></term>
307       <listitem>
308        <para>
309         If specified, corruption checking begins at the specified block,
310         skipping all previous blocks.  It is an error to specify a
311         <parameter>startblock</parameter> outside the range of blocks in the
312         target table.
313        </para>
314        <para>
315         By default, checking begins at the first block.
316        </para>
317       </listitem>
318      </varlistentry>
319      <varlistentry>
320       <term><literal>endblock</literal></term>
321       <listitem>
322        <para>
323         If specified, corruption checking ends at the specified block,
324         skipping all remaining blocks.  It is an error to specify an
325         <parameter>endblock</parameter> outside the range of blocks in the target
326         table.
327        </para>
328        <para>
329         By default, all blocks are checked.
330        </para>
331       </listitem>
332      </varlistentry>
333     </variablelist>
334     <para>
335      For each corruption detected, <function>verify_heapam</function> returns
336      a row with the following columns:
337     </para>
338     <variablelist>
339      <varlistentry>
340       <term><literal>blkno</literal></term>
341       <listitem>
342        <para>
343         The number of the block containing the corrupt page.
344        </para>
345       </listitem>
346      </varlistentry>
347      <varlistentry>
348       <term><literal>offnum</literal></term>
349       <listitem>
350        <para>
351         The OffsetNumber of the corrupt tuple.
352        </para>
353       </listitem>
354      </varlistentry>
355      <varlistentry>
356       <term><literal>attnum</literal></term>
357       <listitem>
358        <para>
359         The attribute number of the corrupt column in the tuple, if the
360         corruption is specific to a column and not the tuple as a whole.
361        </para>
362       </listitem>
363      </varlistentry>
364      <varlistentry>
365       <term><literal>msg</literal></term>
366       <listitem>
367        <para>
368         A message describing the problem detected.

378 <sect2 id="amcheck-optional-heapallindexed-verification">
379  <title>Optional <parameter>heapallindexed</parameter> Verification</title>
380 <para>
381  When the <parameter>heapallindexed</parameter> argument to B-Tree
382  verification functions is <literal>true</literal>, an additional
383  phase of verification is performed against the table associated with
384  the target index relation.  This consists of a <quote>dummy</quote>
385  <command>CREATE INDEX CONCURRENTLY</command> operation, which checks for the
386  presence of all hypothetical new index tuples against a temporary,
387  in-memory summarizing structure (this is built when needed during
388  the basic first phase of verification).  The summarizing structure
389  <quote>fingerprints</quote> every tuple found within the target
390  index.  The high level principle behind
391  <parameter>heapallindexed</parameter> verification is that a new
392  index that is equivalent to the existing, target index must only
393  have entries that can be found in the existing structure.
394 </para>
395 <para>
396  The additional <parameter>heapallindexed</parameter> phase adds
397  significant overhead: verification will typically take several times
398  longer.  However, there is no change to the relation-level locks
399  acquired when <parameter>heapallindexed</parameter> verification is
400  performed.
401 </para>
402 <para>
403  The summarizing structure is bound in size by
404  <varname>maintenance_work_mem</varname>.  In order to ensure that
405  there is no more than a 2% probability of failure to detect an
406  inconsistency for each heap tuple that should be represented in the
407  index, approximately 2 bytes of memory are needed per tuple.  As
408  less memory is made available per tuple, the probability of missing
409  an inconsistency slowly increases.  This approach limits the
410  overhead of verification significantly, while only slightly reducing
411  the probability of detecting a problem, especially for installations
412  where verification is treated as a routine maintenance task.  Any
413  single absent or malformed tuple has a new opportunity to be
414  detected with each new verification attempt.
415 </para>

419 <sect2 id="amcheck-using-amcheck-effectively">
420  <title>Using <filename>amcheck</filename> Effectively</title>
421
422 <para>
423  <filename>amcheck</filename> can be effective at detecting various types of
424  failure modes that <link
425  linkend="app-initdb-data-checksums"><application>data
426  checksums</application></link> will fail to catch.  These include:
427
428  <itemizedlist>
429   <listitem>
430    <para>
431     Structural inconsistencies caused by incorrect operator class
432     implementations.
433    </para>
434    <para>
435     This includes issues caused by the comparison rules of operating
436     system collations changing. Comparisons of datums of a collatable
437     type like <type>text</type> must be immutable (just as all
438     comparisons used for B-Tree index scans must be immutable), which
439     implies that operating system collation rules must never change.
440     Though rare, updates to operating system collation rules can
441     cause these issues. More commonly, an inconsistency in the
442     collation order between a primary server and a standby server is
443     implicated, possibly because the <emphasis>major</emphasis> operating
444     system version in use is inconsistent.  Such inconsistencies will
445     generally only arise on standby servers, and so can generally
446     only be detected on standby servers.
447    </para>
448    <para>
449     If a problem like this arises, it may not affect each individual
450     index that is ordered using an affected collation, simply because
451     <emphasis>indexed</emphasis> values might happen to have the same
452     absolute ordering regardless of the behavioral inconsistency. See
453     <xref linkend="locale"/> and <xref linkend="collation"/> for
454     further details about how <productname>PostgreSQL</productname> uses
455     operating system locales and collations.
456    </para>
457   </listitem>
458   <listitem>
459    <para>
460     Structural inconsistencies between indexes and the heap relations
461     that are indexed (when <parameter>heapallindexed</parameter>
462     verification is performed).
463    </para>
464    <para>
465     There is no cross-checking of indexes against their heap relation
466     during normal operation.  Symptoms of heap corruption can be subtle.
467    </para>
468   </listitem>
469   <listitem>
470    <para>
471     Corruption caused by hypothetical undiscovered bugs in the
472     underlying <productname>PostgreSQL</productname> access method
473     code, sort code, or transaction management code.
474    </para>
475    <para>
476     Automatic verification of the structural integrity of indexes
477     plays a role in the general testing of new or proposed
478     <productname>PostgreSQL</productname> features that could plausibly allow a
479     logical inconsistency to be introduced.  Verification of table
480     structure and associated visibility and transaction status
481     information plays a similar role.  One obvious testing strategy
482     is to call <filename>amcheck</filename> functions continuously
483     when running the standard regression tests.  See <xref
484     linkend="regress-run"/> for details on running the tests.
485    </para>
486   </listitem>
487   <listitem>
488    <para>
489     File system or storage subsystem faults when data checksums are
490     disabled.
491    </para>
492    <para>
493     Note that <filename>amcheck</filename> examines a page as represented in some
494     shared memory buffer at the time of verification if there is only a
495     shared buffer hit when accessing the block. Consequently,
496     <filename>amcheck</filename> does not necessarily examine data read from the
497     file system at the time of verification. Note that when checksums are
498     enabled, <filename>amcheck</filename> may raise an error due to a checksum
499     failure when a corrupt block is read into a buffer.
500    </para>
501   </listitem>
502   <listitem>
503    <para>
504     Corruption caused by faulty RAM, or the broader memory subsystem.
505    </para>
506    <para>
507     <productname>PostgreSQL</productname> does not protect against correctable
508     memory errors and it is assumed you will operate using RAM that
509     uses industry standard Error Correcting Codes (ECC) or better
510     protection.  However, ECC memory is typically only immune to
511     single-bit errors, and should not be assumed to provide
512     <emphasis>absolute</emphasis> protection against failures that
513     result in memory corruption.
514    </para>
515    <para>
516     When <parameter>heapallindexed</parameter> verification is
517     performed, there is generally a greatly increased chance of
518     detecting single-bit errors, since strict binary equality is
519     tested, and the indexed attributes within the heap are tested.
520    </para>
521   </listitem>
522  </itemizedlist>
523 </para>
524
525 <para>
526  Structural corruption can happen due to faulty storage hardware, or
527  relation files being overwritten or modified by unrelated software.
528  This kind of corruption can also be detected with
529  <link linkend="checksums"><application>data page
530  checksums</application></link>.
531 </para>
532
533 <para>
534  Relation pages which are correctly formatted, internally consistent, and
535  correct relative to their own internal checksums may still contain
536  logical corruption.  As such, this kind of corruption cannot be detected
537  with <application>checksums</application>.  Examples include toasted
538  values in the main table which lack a corresponding entry in the toast
539  table, and tuples in the main table with a Transaction ID that is older
540  than the oldest valid Transaction ID in the database or cluster.
541 </para>
542
543 <para>
544  Multiple causes of logical corruption have been observed in production
545  systems, including bugs in the <productname>PostgreSQL</productname>
546  server software, faulty and ill-conceived backup and restore tools, and
547  user error.
548 </para>
549
550 <para>
551  Corrupt relations are most concerning in live production environments,
552  precisely the same environments where high risk activities are least
553  welcome.  For this reason, <function>verify_heapam</function> has been
554  designed to diagnose corruption without undue risk.  It cannot guard
555  against all causes of backend crashes, as even executing the calling
556  query could be unsafe on a badly corrupted system.   Access to <link
557  linkend="catalogs-overview">catalog tables</link> is performed and could
558  be problematic if the catalogs themselves are corrupted.
559 </para>
560
561 <para>
562  In general, <filename>amcheck</filename> can only prove the presence of
563  corruption; it cannot prove its absence.
564 </para>
565
566 </sect2>
567 <sect2 id="amcheck-repairing-corruption">
568  <title>Repairing Corruption</title>
569 <para>
570  No error concerning corruption raised by <filename>amcheck</filename> should
571  ever be a false positive.  <filename>amcheck</filename> raises
572  errors in the event of conditions that, by definition, should never
573  happen, and so careful analysis of <filename>amcheck</filename>
574  errors is often required.
575 </para>
576 <para>
577  There is no general method of repairing problems that
578  <filename>amcheck</filename> detects.  An explanation for the root cause of
579  an invariant violation should be sought.  <xref
580  linkend="pageinspect"/> may play a useful role in diagnosing
581  corruption that <filename>amcheck</filename> detects.  A <command>REINDEX</command>
582  may not be effective in repairing corruption.
583 </para>
